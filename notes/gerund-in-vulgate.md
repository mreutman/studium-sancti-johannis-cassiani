## Is “gerund + accusative” Classical Latin?

In a word: **no**.

Classical rule (Cicero, Caesar, Livy):

* A **gerund does not take a direct object**.
* If there would be an object, Latin strongly prefers a **gerundive construction**.

So instead of:

> x *in legendō librum*

Classical Latin requires:

> + *in legendō librō*

The object is absorbed into the gerundive phrase.

So constructions like:

> *in deficiendo spiritum meum*

are **not Classical Latin syntax**.

---

## What Is Happening in the Vulgate?

Jerome is imitating the Greek construction:

> ἐν τῷ ἐκλείπειν τὸ πνεῦμά μου

Greek:

* ἐν + article + infinitive
* infinitive freely takes its own accusative subject

Jerome wants to preserve:

* the temporal framing
* the verbal energy
* the accusative subject feel

Latin has no articular infinitive.
The closest structural equivalent available is:

> in + gerund

But the gerund normally blocks a direct object — so Jerome stretches Latin to approximate the Greek.

So yes:

This is a **Greek syntactic calque**.

---

## Is This “Medieval Latin”?

Not exactly.

This phenomenon belongs to:

### **Biblical Latin / Christian Latin**

(4th–5th century onward)

It is not a natural development of spoken Latin.
It is a **translation-induced phenomenon**.

In other words:

* It did not arise organically in Latin prose.
* It arose from bilingual Greek–Latin theological culture.

---

## Was This Unique to Jerome?

No — but he is one of its most influential users.

### Before Jerome

The **Old Latin translations** already showed heavy Greek interference.

### After Jerome

This style becomes part of the biblical and liturgical register.

You see similar Greek-influenced constructions in:

* Tertullian (earlier but Greek-influenced Christian Latin)
* Hilary of Poitiers
* Augustine of Hippo (occasionally)
* Later medieval biblical commentaries

However — and this is important —

Outside biblical and theological writing, the construction does **not** become standard Latin.

You will not find secular medieval historians routinely using gerund + accusative.

It remains largely a **biblical register feature**.

---

## Did It Become Fully Accepted?

It becomes tolerated within:

* Scriptural Latin
* Liturgical Latin
* Texts consciously echoing Scripture

But grammarians of the classical tradition would still consider it irregular.

Even medieval school grammars continue teaching the classical gerund rule.

So this construction lives in a kind of sacred stylistic enclave.

---

## What It Represents Linguistically

This is a textbook example of:

> **syntactic borrowing under translation pressure**

It shows:

* Greek prestige
* bilingual clerical culture
* the desire to preserve perceived inspired structure

It is not a “natural evolution” of Latin grammar —
it is a **translation artifact that became conventionalized in sacred style**.

---

## The Vulgate Form

Psalm 141:4:

> **In deficiendo ex me spiritum meum**

This mirrors the Greek:

> ἐν τῷ ἐκλείπειν ἐξ ἐμοῦ τὸ πνεῦμά μου

Functionally:

> “When my spirit was failing within me”

Despite the accusative (*spiritum meum*), the meaning is straightforwardly **intransitive**:

* *spiritus meus deficit*
* “my spirit fails”

The gerund here is verbal and event-focused.

It describes the occurrence of the failing.

---

## If We Recast It as a Gerundive

Classical Latin would prefer:

> **In deficiente ex me spiritu meo**

Now the structure changes:

* *deficiente* = present participle/gerundive-like ablative
* *spiritu meo* = ablative absolute

This is now essentially an ablative absolute construction:

> “with my spirit failing within me”

---

## Does the Meaning Change?

Not dramatically — but subtly, yes.

### A. Gerund Version:

> in deficiendo …
> Event-centered.
> Focus on the **process of failing**.

### B. Gerundive / Participial Version:

> in deficiente spiritu meo
> State-centered.
> Focus on the **condition of my spirit as failing**.

So:

* The gerund emphasizes the action.
* The gerundive/participle emphasizes the state accompanying the action.

---

## Theological or Emotional Difference?

Very slight, but perceptible.

Greek aorist infinitive:

> ἐκλείπειν
> has event force — “as it was failing” / “when it failed”

The Latin gerund keeps that verbal dynamism.

The ablative absolute slightly stabilizes it into background circumstance.

It feels less dramatic.

---

## Why Jerome Might Have Preferred the Gerund

Jerome is preserving the **verbal energy** of the Greek infinitive construction.

The gerund allows:

* event framing
* temporal force
* closer structural imitation of ἐν τῷ + infinitive

The ablative absolute is smoother Latin — but less Greek.

It would smooth Jerome’s Greek edge.
